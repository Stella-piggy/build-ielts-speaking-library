# Synthetic Examples

All facts below are fictional test data. Never copy them into a learner's library.

## Example 1: Duplicate prompts and one reusable story

Question bank excerpt:

- `Describe a person who encouraged you to achieve a goal.`
- `Describe someone who helped you achieve something.`
- `Describe a difficult thing you did successfully.`

Correct handling:

1. Keep all three exact questions.
2. Keep the first two in separate duplicate groups because `encouraged` and `helped` require different actions. They may share a people-and-achievement cluster and candidate story.
3. Keep the third in a separate duplicate group linked to the same possible story cluster.
4. Ask one module question about a real challenge, the goal, the helper, actions, result, feelings, and reflection.
5. Present candidate facts for confirmation.
6. After confirmation, create one story card and map:
   - helper prompts: `自然覆盖` if the helper's role is central
   - difficult achievement: `自然覆盖` if the difficulty and actions are fully known
   - a prompt about `a person you disliked at first`: `不建议使用` unless that relationship fact is also confirmed

The common story does not erase different recommended angles.

`Describe a useful skill you learned` and `Describe a skill someone taught you` follow the same rule: separate task groups, potentially one cluster and one confirmed story.

## Example 2: Short answer and interview budget

Main answer: `去年我学了摄影，挺难的。`

Correct sequence:

1. Follow-up 1 asks for the most informative missing event detail: what made it difficult and what the learner actually did.
2. If the reply is still thin, follow-up 2 asks for the result and one concrete moment or feeling.
3. Stop after two follow-ups.
4. Show supplied facts as unconfirmed candidates.
5. After confirmation, write only those facts; mark absent person, turning point, or reflection as `未提供`.

Incorrect handling: asking for difficulty, helper, location, result, emotion, and reflection all at once after the short answer, or inventing a photography contest, teacher, camera model, or award.

## Example 3: Contradiction and no suitable material

Existing confirmed card: `The learner first visited Hangzhou in 2022 with a university friend.`

New answer: `I first went there alone in 2023.`

Correct handling:

1. Do not overwrite the existing card.
2. Quote both claims briefly and ask whether one is wrong or whether they refer to different trips or meanings of `first`.
3. Update or split the card only after explicit confirmation.

If the bank asks for `a place damaged by pollution` and neither trip contains confirmed pollution details, map it as `不建议使用`. Explain that environmental damage is the missing central requirement and offer a focused interview. Do not add litter, smog, or a cleanup event.

## Example 4: Real difference between answer versions

Confirmed synthetic facts: the learner used to avoid cooking; learned tomato-and-egg stir-fry from a roommate during lockdown; the first attempt was too salty; later it became a weekly habit and made the learner more independent.

For `Describe a skill you learned`, Version A might follow a simple chronology with common links: prior situation → roommate's help → salty first attempt → repeated practice → independence.

Version B should retain every fact but develop why the mistake mattered, connect the turning point naturally, paraphrase `learned` contextually, and use a controlled structure such as a relative clause or short `what...was...` emphasis pattern. Use a conditional only when its causal claim is confirmed. It should not merely replace `difficult` with `arduous` or add unrelated idioms.

Do not add a plausible contrast such as `I never wanted to become a chef` unless the learner confirmed it. Version B cannot buy sophistication with invented biography.

A valid comparison names concrete changes:

- logic: Version B explains how repeated cooking led to independence
- cohesion: `What changed things was...` links the failed attempt to practice
- wording: `get the seasoning right` is more precise than repeating `cook well`
- structure: a short conditional reflection adds perspective without making the answer hard to reproduce

## Example 5: Practice correction without style erasure

Learner sample: `I very like cooking because it makes me relax, and I always cook cooking at weekend.`

Feedback priorities:

1. It answers the preference question.
2. The reason is clear but the example is undeveloped.
3. Fix meaning/accuracy: `I really like cooking`; `it helps me relax`; `I usually cook at weekends/on the weekend`.
4. Explain the repetition of `cook/cooking` and offer a contextual alternative such as `make a simple meal`.
5. Minimal edit: preserve the original two-idea structure.
6. Upgrade: add one confirmed example only; do not invent a favorite dish.
7. Extract at most 3-5 useful phrases.

## Example 6: High-density interview without card collapse

One answer may state that schools should teach first aid, parents should teach cooking, and learners give up when progress is invisible. This is efficient evidence collection, but it supports three central claims.

Correct handling:

- keep the three question tasks in separate duplicate groups
- keep every group visible in the interview plan
- create separate viewpoint cards for school responsibility, parent responsibility, and causes of giving up
- cross-reference one confirmed personal example where relevant
- write `现实例子：未提供` when the answer contains only reasons, recommendations, or hypothetical illustrations; do not relabel them as real examples

Incorrect handling: calling all three prompts one duplicate group or writing one oversized viewpoint card whose stance changes subject and question function.

## Example 7: Three-part answer calibration

Use the same fact ledger for A and B.

### Part 1 preference

Confirmed facts: likes cooking now; makes tomato-and-egg stir-fry at weekends; cooking saves money and helps the learner relax; started at university.

- A: answer directly, add the weekend dish and one or two reasons in 2-4 sentences.
- B: add the confirmed past-to-present change and use a common chunk such as `a home-cooked meal` or `wind down`; do not turn the answer into a speech.
- Bad upgrade: replacing `relax` with an obscure adjective while leaving the logic unchanged.

### Part 2 experience

Confirmed facts: Ajie taught histogram use; exposure was difficult; the learner practised in a park and later took properly exposed graduation photos for a sister.

- A: use a clear chronological arc and accurate common language.
- B: improve the turning point, paraphrase `useful` as `came in handy`, and add one controlled relative or emphasis structure.
- Keep B close enough to A that both can use one outline. Do not add `I wasn't trying to become a professional photographer`, a stronger family emotion, or a technical consequence that was never confirmed.
- For `Describe a skill someone taught you`, omit the confirmed fact that Ajie did not encourage a separate goal; it is relevant to an encouragement mapping, not this answer.

### Part 3 opinion

Confirmed facts: schools should teach first aid and basic money management; a real two-hour first-aid workshop included a CPR demonstration; core subjects must keep their time; families can teach cooking and housework.

- A: position → reason → real example → qualification.
- B: make the school/family division clearer and keep the learning load concentrated in a small set such as `structured guidance`, `a busy timetable`, and `share the responsibility`.
- Bad upgrade: stacking `be well placed to`, `financial literacy`, `squeeze into`, and `at the expense of` in one short response merely to sound advanced.

### Required four-criterion comparison

- **流畅与连贯:** compare script organization and cohesive progression; do not infer real-time fluency.
- **词汇资源:** show precise contextual paraphrase, not a rare-word list.
- **语法范围与准确性:** show useful controlled variety; neither version contains intentional errors.
- **发音:** with text only, state that pronunciation is not scored and list the unavailable audio features.
- **事实一致性:** confirm that both versions use the same people, time, events, reasons, results, and stance.

## Example 8: Targeted spoken polish after learner feedback

Synthetic situation: a learner gives a detailed answer about a university case competition. The answer is accurate and personal, but the learner says a few phrases sound written. After one retelling, the learner explicitly says the original full version is better than a heavily shortened rewrite.

Correct handling:

1. Restore the preferred full version and preserve its story arc, technical detail, and emotional ending.
2. Edit only the phrases that are genuinely stiff or hard to retrieve, for example:
   - `control the time` → `stay within the time limit` or `finish on time`
   - `maintain the quality` → `keep the quality high` or `make sure our ideas were clear`
   - an overloaded responsibility sentence → two natural spoken clauses
3. Keep accurate domain language such as `financial statements` when it identifies the real task.
4. Treat `sophomore year` as natural American English, not an error. Prefer `in my second year at university` when the learner wants a more internationally transparent everyday option.
5. Use automatic-transcript failures only as possible retrieval signals. Do not label them pronunciation errors without decoded audio.

Incorrect handling: deleting the business details, replacing the story with generic short sentences, or assuming that every multi-syllable word recognized incorrectly must be removed.
