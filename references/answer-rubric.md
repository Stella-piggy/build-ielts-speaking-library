## 1. Evidence and target labels

Use only confirmed library facts and facts explicitly supplied for the current answer. Freeze a small task-specific fact ledger before drafting and compare both versions against it afterward. Remove any unsupported ambition, motive, emotion, consequence, relationship, example, or negative contrast. Do not widen the time scope of a fact: `I couldn't find a reference case for this task` does not mean `I had never seen such a case before`.

Treat `5.5-6` and `6.5-7` as target difficulty labels, not predicted scores. Never add errors, hesitation, repetition, or awkwardness to imitate a lower band.

## 2. Required selected-question output

Use these nine sections in order:

1. **中文回答逻辑** — compact content path, not a full translation.
2. **英文关键词提纲** — keywords and short recall chunks.
3. **版本A｜5.5—6分稳定表达版** — complete, direct, and speakable.
4. **版本B｜6.5—7分灵活自然版** — more flexible but still repeatable.
5. **两个版本的具体差异** — compare the four IELTS Speaking criteria and fact consistency.
6. **值得积累的自然表达** — only 3-5 expressions.
7. **短语含义与适用语境** — Chinese meaning and realistic use.
8. **可迁移表达** — only mappings that remain truthful.
9. **本次练习最需要注意的一点** — one prioritized action.

Run the persistence checkpoint in `SKILL.md` after section 9.

## 3. Version A and Version B

### Version A: 5.5-6 stable expression

- Answer the exact task and cover every cue-card obligation.
- Use a visible sequence such as setting → difficulty → action → result → feeling.
- Prefer common connectors and everyday verbs, but keep precise domain nouns when they are necessary to the learner's real story, such as `financial statements` or `business model`.
- Mix clear simple sentences with a few manageable complex sentences.
- Keep enough concrete detail to sound personal. Do not make the answer childish, generic, or unnaturally short.
- Preserve correct grammar; never manufacture mistakes.

### Version B: 6.5-7 flexible natural expression

- Use the same fact ledger and broadly the same story or argument.
- Improve development, transitions, contextual paraphrase, collocation, and controlled grammar variety.
- Prefer natural spoken progression such as `At first`, `However`, `In the end`, or meaning-driven transitions over formal essay linkers and mechanical lists.
- Add a useful detail or reflection only when confirmed or directly entailed.
- Keep the length close enough to A that the learner can practise both from one outline.
- Do not stack rare words, ornamental clauses, idioms, or regional slang.

The difference must be functional, not a synonym exercise: B should connect and develop ideas more flexibly while remaining realistic to say.

## 4. Personal spoken-style calibration

Use evidence in this order:

1. the learner's real spoken sample or reliable transcript;
2. the learner's explicit feedback about preferred answers;
3. the learner's original written sample;
4. general target-level defaults.

Treat direct learner feedback as decisive. If the learner prefers an earlier, fuller version, preserve its structure and detail and make only the requested polish. Do not replace the whole answer with short generic sentences merely because one retelling contained hesitation or transcription noise.

When the learner says an answer feels written, first identify the exact causes and edit locally:

- replace an unnecessarily formal linker or noun phrase;
- split one overloaded sentence;
- use a more conversational verb;
- keep important personal and professional detail;
- retain a direct final feeling or reflection.

Spoken English does not mean every sentence must be short. A clear medium-length sentence is acceptable when it follows the learner's demonstrated rhythm.

Use internationally intelligible wording. Region-specific expressions may be natural but should not be the automatic default. For example, `sophomore year` is normal American English, while `in my second year at university` is more widely transparent. Use the learner's preferred variety or offer the simpler alternative without calling the original wrong.

Before presenting an answer, check:

- **Fact and relevance:** every claim is confirmed and helps answer the prompt.
- **Spoken rhythm:** the answer sounds like a person telling a story or explaining a view, not reading a report or essay.
- **Retrievability:** difficult wording is concentrated in a few useful phrases, not spread across every sentence.
- **Personal voice:** the answer keeps the learner's detail, emotional emphasis, and preferred organization.

## 5. Four-criterion comparison

In section 5, use four rows:

1. **流畅与连贯** — for text, compare relevance, sequence, development, and cohesion only. Do not infer actual speed, hesitation, repetition, or self-correction.
2. **词汇资源** — compare precision, appropriacy, collocation, and contextual paraphrase using excerpts from both versions. Rewrite B if it merely swaps in rarer words.
3. **语法范围与准确性** — compare useful controlled structures. A contains no deliberate errors; B does not gain range by piling up clauses.
4. **发音** — with text only, write exactly: `未评分：当前只有文字，无法判断清晰度、语块切分、重音、节奏、语调、连读或实际可理解度。`

Then add one **事实一致性** line confirming that both versions use the same relevant facts.

## 6. Part-specific controls

### Part 1

Answer directly and personally, normally with a reason or small example. Use profile cards. Avoid a mini-speech.

### Part 2

Use a coherent story arc and cover every cue-card obligation without reading them as a checklist. Use story cards. Preserve specific details that make the answer recognizably the learner's; simplify only the phrases that create unnecessary delivery pressure.

### Part 3

Use position → reason → real example → qualification or conclusion. Use a viewpoint card for the central claim. A personal story may support the argument but cannot replace it.

## 7. Practice review

When the learner submits an answer, respond in this order:

1. Decide whether it answers the question and identify missed obligations.
2. Check logic and connections.
3. Mark language problems that affect meaning or accuracy.
4. Identify repetition and stiff or translation-like wording.
5. Produce a minimal-edit version that preserves the learner's structure and wording.
6. Produce a 6.5-7 spoken upgrade.
7. Extract only 3-5 expressions with Chinese meaning and use context.
8. State one highest-priority next action.

Do not overhaul the whole answer when a few local edits solve the problem. If the learner says the original answer was better, restore it and keep only improvements the learner accepts.

Treat automatic transcripts cautiously. Misspellings and recognition failures are not proof of pronunciation or grammar errors. Evaluate pronunciation only when the environment genuinely decodes and plays the audio; otherwise state the limitation. Never claim an official score.

Update the language-style record from stable evidence: recurring successful structures, repeated retrieval problems, and explicit learner preferences. Do not infer style from age, major, gender, or personality labels.

## 8. Output control

- one selected question at a time unless the learner explicitly requests a small comparison set;
- one main practice priority per round;
- only 3-5 expressions;
- concise version comparisons using actual excerpts;
- no batch model answers.
